Introduction & Motivation

Food insecurity refers to the limited or uncertain availability of nutritious, affordable food. It does not only describe whether people have food in the moment, but whether they can reliably access healthy food options that support long-term well-being. Across the United States, food insecurity is strongly shaped by structural factors such as neighborhood poverty, access to transportation, distance to supermarkets, and broader socioeconomic conditions. These factors often combine to produce food deserts, where residents must travel long distances usually without a personal vehicle to reach a grocery store.

To study these issues at a meaningful level of detail, this project uses census tracts as the primary unit of analysis. A census tract is a small, relatively permanent geographic region defined by the U.S. Census Bureau, typically containing 2,5008,000 residents. Tracts are designed to represent neighborhoods with similar demographic and socioeconomic characteristics. Census tracts allow for a more precise analysis of community-level differences in income, transportation access, and food availability.

A key metric in this project is the low-access population, defined by the USDA Food Access Research Atlas. A resident is considered low-access if they live beyond a reasonable distance from a supermarket: urban tracts (more than 1 mile from a supermarket) and rural tracts (more than 10 miles from a supermarket)

Why this matters for data science: Understanding food insecurity requires integrating heterogeneous datasets (tract-level, county-level, health outcomes, socioeconomic indicators) and using data science tools to clean, merge, visualize, and model them. This tutorial demonstrates real-world challenges in data wrangling, geographic identifiers (FIPS), multi-scale data alignment, exploratory analysis, and predictive modeling. Food insecurity is a spatial, structural, and statistical problemmaking it a rich data science case study.

The project focuses on the following research questions:

  • How is census-tract or county-level food insecurity distributed across the United States, and what does the variation in its distribution reveal about geographic inequality?
  • What is the relationship between structural food-access barriers and food insecurity at the county level?
  • How do socioeconomic and geographic factors shape food insecurity rates across U.S. counties?

Addressing these questions through data cleaning, integration, exploratory analysis, and early modeling will prepare the foundation for building predictive models in the next milestone.

Data Sources

This project integrates three large public datasets to analyze neighborhood-level food access and broader county-level food insecurity conditions across the United States.

  1. USDA Food Access Research Atlas (Census Tract Level)
  • Unit: Census tract
  • Source: USDA ERS
  • Purpose: Measures physical access to supermarkets and identifies low-access populations.
  • Link: https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-access-research-atlas/
  1. USDA Food Environment Atlas (County Level)
  • Unit: County
  • Source: USDA ERS
  • Purpose: Describes socioeconomic and community factors shaping county-level food security.
  • Link: https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/food-environment-atlas/
  1. CDC PLACES (Census Tract & County Level)
  • Unit: Census tract & county
  • Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention
  • Purpose: Provides modeled health-related measures for small-area surveillance, including Food Insecurity (Crude Prevalence).
  • Link: https://www.cdc.gov/places/

Together, the USDA Food Access Atlas, USDA Food Environment Atlas, and CDC PLACES datasets provide tract and county-level measures of food access, socioeconomic conditions, and modeled food insecurity prevalence to allow for analysis of the structural and geographic factors shaping food insecurity across the U.S.

3. Handle missing values

To keep computed rates meaningful:

  • I drop tracts with missing Pop2010 (population is required for any rate).
  • I fill missing low-access counts (lapop1, lalowi1, lahunv1) with 0.
  • I then recompute the share variables as counts divided by population, so they are consistent and reflect the cleaned counts.
In[5]:
# Check missing values before cleaning
fa.isna().sum().sort_values(ascending=False)

5. Aggregate tract-level data to the county level

The Food Environment Atlas and CDC PLACES datasets are defined at the county level.
To merge with those sources later, I aggregate tract-level counts into county-level totals:

  • total_pop: sum of tract populations in the county
  • total_low_access: total residents with low access
  • total_low_income_low_access: total low-income residents with low access
  • total_no_vehicle_low_access: total low-access residents in households without a vehicle

Then I convert these totals into county-level rates by dividing by total_pop.

In[7]:
# Each row in fa_clean is a census tract; aggregate by county_fips
fa_county = fa_clean.groupby("county_fips").agg(
    total_pop=("Pop2010", "sum"),
    total_low_access=("lapop1", "sum"),
    total_low_income_low_access=("lalowi1", "sum"),
    total_no_vehicle_low_access=("lahunv1", "sum")
).reset_index()

# County-level rates
fa_county["low_access_rate_county"] = (
    fa_county["total_low_access"] / fa_county["total_pop"]
)

fa_county["low_income_low_access_rate_county"] = (
    fa_county["total_low_income_low_access"] / fa_county["total_pop"]
)

fa_county["no_vehicle_low_access_rate_county"] = (
    fa_county["total_no_vehicle_low_access"] / fa_county["total_pop"]
)

Modeling Step 2 Fit a Multiple Linear Regression Model

With the modeling dataset prepared, the next step is to fit a multiple linear regression model to evaluate how structural county-level factors relate to food insecurity.

Linear regression is used here because:

  • The outcome variable (food_insecurity_rate) is continuous.
  • Coefficients are easy to interpret and show how each predictor is associated with food insecurity.
  • It provides a clear baseline model before exploring more complex approaches.

In this step, I:

  1. Split the dataset into training (80%) and testing (20%) sets.
  2. Fit a linear regression model using the selected predictors.
  3. Generate predictions on the test set.
  4. Compute evaluation metrics (including Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and R )to assess model performance.

from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

Conclusion

This project combined USDA Food Access Atlas, Food Environment Atlas, and CDC PLACES data to examine structural drivers of food insecurity across U.S. counties. The EDA showed a highly uneven, right-skewed distribution of food insecurity, with higher poverty levels reliably corresponding to higher food insecurity.

The linear regression model (R ~0.25) confirmed these patterns: the largest predictor was the share of residents in low-access households without a vehicle, followed by county poverty rate. Distance-based access measures had weaker effects once socioeconomic factors were included.

Overall, food insecurity is shaped by intertwined economic, geographic, and transportation barriers. Improving supermarket access alone is insufficient. Addressing poverty and broader structural inequities is essential for meaningful reduction in food insecurity.

Geographies of Food Access and Food Insecurity Academic Literature Review

These resources provide foundational and advanced insights into how economic inequality, geography, transportation, retail structure, and policy environments jointly shape food insecurity. They also offer expanded datasets and modeling approaches for future research or extended versions of this project.
